Frequently Asked Questions

How many NASCAR teams are there?

There are 21 full-time organizations in the NASCAR Rocky Mountain Regional Series.

What makes up a NASCAR team?

A NASCAR organization is limited to four cars, similar to that of the three NASCAR national series. Teams mostly utilize one manufacturer for all of their cars, but have a different car number and crew chief for each driver. Different teams that share the same manufacturer often work together in an alliance. For example, Wood Brothers Racing has a technical alliance with Team Penske.

How many teams can a NASCAR owner have?

There is no strict limit beyond the four-car limit per organization enforced by the series; owners may hold interests in multiple organizations so long as series rules are followed.

Common Terms and Concepts

Learn the terminology, rules, and concepts of stock car racing.

Common Racing Terms

Pit Stop

A scheduled stop where the crew services the vehicle—changing tires, adding fuel, and making repairs. Pit stops are critical to race strategy and can make or break a good result.

Drafting

A technique where a driver follows closely behind another vehicle to reduce wind resistance, allowing faster speeds on straightaways. Typically seen on superspeedways (Daytona and Talladega).

Lapping

When the leader(s) lap a slower car, meaning they complete one more full lap than that car. Lapped traffic can significantly affect race dynamics, as drivers have to navigate through traffic while still holding off faster cars.

Yellow Flag / Caution

Displayed when there's an incident that is potentially hazardous (crash, debris, weather). Drivers must slow down to pit road speed (varies track-to-track), and typically no passing is allowed until the green flag returns. However, drivers who pit, have mechanical issues, or receive a penalty during a caution period can be passed.

Green Flag

Signals the start of a race or the resumption of racing after a caution period.

Red Flag

Stops the race entirely due to severe weather, major accidents, or track hazards. All drivers must return to pit lane and the race is temporarily halted.

Checkered Flag

Waves to signify the end of the race. The first driver to cross the finish line under the checkered flag is the winner.

Points Leader

The driver currently with the most championship points. Points are awarded based on finishing position in each race.

Pole Position

The first starting position in a race, usually earned by the driver with the fastest qualifying lap, unless qualifying is rained out or cancelled for any reason.

Bump and Run

A controversial racing maneuver where a driver intentionally lightly taps the rear of a competitor to move them aside, often used in short track racing. This lifts the rear tires of the car in front off the ground, allowing the pursuing driver to be temporarily faster and have more grip.

Racing Rules & Penalties

Contact Penalties

Drivers can be penalized for intentional contact, but other than that NASCAR is pretty chill about officiatiating crashes, as this is closed fender racing where contact is ineviteble, but the cars are built to withstand light contact to a point. Penalties may include disqualification or championship points deductions.

Speeding on Pit Road

Drivers must maintain a strict speed limit on pit road (typically 35-55 mph depending on the track). Violations under green flag conditions result in a pass-through penalty where drivers have to come through pit road at pit road speed. Penalties under caution mean being sent to the back of the field (end of the longest line) for the restart.

Equipment Failure

If a car experiences mechanical issues (engine failure, transmission problems, tire issues, etc), the driver may need to come down pit road to fix the issue, and for more intense issues, go to the garage and exit the race. NASCAR will black flag a car if they are dropping debris on the track, such as fluid leaks or tire fragments.

Fuel Mileage Strategy

Drivers must manage fuel consumption throughout the race. Teams will often employ strategies such as saving fuel during caution periods to gain track position or drafting behind other cars to improve fuel efficiency. The more fuel a car has, the longer it can stay out on track before needing to pit. However, this also makes the car much heavier, reducing speed and braking efficiency.

Car Setup & Performance

Tire Wear

Tires degrade over time during a race. Teams must balance when to pit for fresh tires versus staying out and maintaining or gaining track position. This is a risky strategy call, as staying out too long can without changing tires can lead to getting easily outperformed by cars behind the car that didn't pit for tires. Lightning McQueen and his tire blowout was not too far off from reality.

Handling

Refers to how well the car responds to the driver's inputs. "Tight" means the front end doesn't turn well, but much easier to control; "loose" means the rear slides excessively, but the car is easier to corner and typically faster. A driver may adjust their driving setup to their liking.

Downforce

Aerodynamic force that pushes the car down, improving traction and cornering speed. More downforce helps handling but reduces top speed. Too much downforce can also reduce fuel efficiency.

Race Positions & Standings

Championship Points

Points are awarded based on finishing position (1st place = 55 points, 35 points awarded to 2nd place, 34 awarded to 3rd, down through the field). The top 10 in the race stages will recieve bonus points based on their finishing position, from 1st = 10 down to 10th getting 1 point. The driver with the fastest lap in the race recieves 1 bonus point.

DNF (Did Not Finish)

A driver who does not cross the finish line at race end due to mechanical failure, accident, or other reasons. A DNF is not good for a driver, because they are guaranteed to get less points because of the poor finish.

Chase Format

The top 16 drivers after the end of the first 20 races (The Regular Season) have their points reset to the 2000 point baseline and are advanced into the NASCAR Chase for The Rocky Mountain Series Cup, with bonus points being awarded based on finishing position in the regular season. The final 10 races are run as normal, however, only the top 16 in points can mathematically win the championship. This is why it is such a big deal to make the Chase.
